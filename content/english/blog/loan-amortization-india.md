## Loan Amortization Basics

Loan amortization is something most people get puzzled by. If you've ever taken out a home loan or a car loan, you might have noticed that the earlier EMIs (Equated Monthly Installments) seem to mostly go towards paying off interest, not the principal. It can feel unfair, especially when you're shelling out hard-earned money every month. But there's a method to the madness, and understanding how loan amortization works can be illuminating.

When you take out a loan, payments are generally structured so that at first, most of the money you pay each month goes towards interest. It's only towards the later years that your payments start tackling the principal more aggressively. This happens because the interest amount is calculated on the outstanding principal. Hence, if Priya from Pune is paying ₹20,000 EMI on her ₹20 lakh home loan, she'd initially find most of her payment going towards interest rather than reducing her loan amount directly.

## The Amortization Calculation

Calculating loan amortization isn't as complicated as it seems. There's a formula at play, and once understood, it adds clarity. The EMI is calculated using a formula involving the principal (P), the rate of interest per month (r), and the number of payments (n). Keep your calculator handy.

## Why It Feels So Uneven

Many are surprised when they realize the interest is front-loaded. Why does this happen? The phenomenon occurs due to the amortization structure itself. Initially, the principal amount is high; thus, the calculated interest based on the principal is also high. Over time, as the remaining principal reduces, the interest on it decreases too, allowing more of the EMI to go towards paying off the principal.

Suppose your bank offers flexible prepayment. You might consider using any salary hike or bonus to repay lump sums, thus trimming down your interest burden. This is why understanding amortization is empowering, it gives you control over your debt management.

## Prepayment and Its Effects

Prepayments are often the savior in such lopsided repayment schedules. Making even a small prepayment can significantly reduce the interest burden and shorten the loan tenure. What's interesting is that most Indian banks allow you to prepay a certain percentage of your loan annually without any penalties. If you're lucky like my colleague Arjun in Bengaluru, with a tech salary "hike" every year, this is something to consider seriously.

It's not just about saving a few rupees each month; it's about optimizing the loan. SIP karo into your debt repayment, if you will. What few realize is that every rupee pre-paid cuts both interest and years from the loan. With an EMI calculator, you can see exactly how much you might save in interest over time.

## Practical Tips for Loan Amortization

There's no one-size-fits-all advice, but here are some practical tips to make the most out of your loan strategy. First, always plan for prepayments. Even before taking out a loan, probe into the prepayment penalties, if any, and understand the terms. Second, keep an eye on the interest rates; refinancing later might be beneficial if rates drop significantly.

Lastly, try and align your payments with your salary cycle. It makes budgeting easier and can prevent skipped payments. Remember, skipping EMIs can dent your CIBIL score, complicating future loan prospects.
