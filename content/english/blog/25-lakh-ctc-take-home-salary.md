Rohit landed his dream job in Bengaluru last April, 25 lakh CTC on the offer letter, and dreams of moving out of his PG to a nice 2BHK in Indiranagar. His family went wild. But when the first salary SMS came, he stared at the number and then at his offer letter. “Bhai, 25 lakh CTC bola tha, yeh 1.5 lakh per month kahan se aaya?” Every Indian techie has this moment. There are memes about it. But seriously, between that glossy CTC and the SMS you get from HDFC every month, five layers of deductions quietly shrink your take-home. Let’s break it down, step by step, with all the actual numbers for FY 2025-26.

## 1. The five layers between offer letter and bank account

First, CTC starts big. The HR presentation at onboarding will talk about 25 lakh CTC. On paper, it feels like you’re set for life. But there’s a reason nobody actually gets this amount in cash.

The first layer is “hidden” employer costs, like employer PF and gratuity. These are paid into EPF or kept aside, not added to your bank account. Many freshers think employer PF is a bonus—it isn’t, it’s part of your CTC already.

Next, there’s the gross salary, which is what payroll actually pays. It’s the CTC minus employer-side deductions. Don’t ignore this number, because every monthly salary slip starts here.

Third, employee-side deductions hit: your own EPF contribution, professional tax, and of course, income tax. These are chopped off before anything lands in your account. Karnataka PT is tiny, just ₹200/month, but income tax at this level is brutal.

Fourth, there are sometimes “recoveries” for things like group insurance premiums or canteen charges, but those are smaller and company-specific.

Last, the amount you actually receive is your net take-home salary. That’s the SMS you forward to your parents. Suddenly, that 25 lakh figure is down to something like ₹1.5 lakh per month. It isn’t a scam. It’s just how Indian payroll is structured.

## 2. What CTC actually contains

CTC isn’t just salary. It’s a loaded number, stuffed with components HR can legally count. Here’s what’s sitting inside your 25 lakh CTC:

Basic salary is the anchor, always 40-50% of CTC in India. For 25 lakh, it’s ₹12,50,000. Everything else is calculated off this. PF, HRA, gratuity—each piggybacks on the basic.

HRA is next. In non-metro cities, it’s usually 40% of basic, so ₹5,00,000. In Mumbai, Delhi, Chennai, Kolkata, it’s 50%. HRA is not always fully tax-free. If you rent, use the [HRA exemption calculator](/salary/hra-calculator/) to check how much is exempt.

Special allowance is the “filler” that HR uses to balance the equation. Whatever is left after basic, HRA, PF, gratuity, gets dumped here. For 25 lakh CTC, it’s a chunky ₹4,26,463.

Employer PF is 12% of basic, or ₹1,50,000. This is deposited into your EPF account, matched by your own contribution.

Gratuity is 4.81% of basic, or ₹60,125. It’s an accounting provision, not cash in hand, and you only get it if you stay for five years.

Legally, your annual gross is what payroll actually pays: ₹23,29,875 in this case. The rest is employer-side benefits and statutory payments. Most people miss this distinction, but it makes the biggest difference in your take-home.

## 4. How the regime choice changes take-home

Tax regime choice is the second-most important factor after CTC split. At 25 lakh CTC, you’re deep into the 20% and even 25% income tax slabs. Under the new regime, standard deduction is allowed, but no Section 80C, no HRA exemption, nothing extra.

If you go for the old regime, you can claim deductions—80C for EPF, ELSS, PPF up to ₹1.5 lakh, HRA exemption if you rent, maybe NPS 80CCD(1B), and Section 80D for health insurance. But most people don’t max out every section, and sometimes the new regime is simpler.

Here’s how the two compare, assuming you claim full 80C (₹1,50,000), standard deduction, ₹2,00,000 HRA exemption, and ₹25,000 under 80D for insurance:

|                        | New Regime (₹) | Old Regime (₹) |
|------------------------|:--------------:|:--------------:|
| Taxable Income         | 22,54,875      | 18,79,875      |
| Income Tax             | 3,51,000       | 2,82,500       |
| Take-home (annual)     | 18,26,475      | 18,94,975      |
| Take-home (monthly)    | 1,52,205       | 1,57,915       |

Old regime gives you about ₹5,700 more per month *if* you claim all the deductions, especially HRA. If you don’t invest or don’t have rent receipts, new regime is less paperwork. For a full breakdown, check our [old vs new tax regime article](/blog/old-vs-new-tax-regime-fy-2025-26/). Most people get this wrong—run the numbers before you pick.

## 5. Five things HR won’t tell you

Variable pay is not guaranteed. That 25 lakh CTC might include a ₹2 lakh “performance bonus” that’s only paid if you hit targets. If you don’t, your actual in-hand could be even lower.

EPF is taxable on employer’s side *after* 7.5 lakh annual employer contribution across all jobs. At 25 lakh, you’re safe, but if you have high NPS or superannuation, watch out.

Gratuity isn’t cash until you finish five years at the same company. HR puts it in your CTC, but don’t expect it as in-hand or even as part of your full and final if you leave early. My ex-colleague Priya in Pune got a shock when she left after four years and realized she’d never see that ₹60,000.

Perks like Sodexo, food coupons, or medical insurance top-ups are not always included in CTC. Sometimes they’re extra, sometimes counted in the “flexi basket.”

Appraisal hikes often only apply to basic, not to special allowance or perks, so your next year’s hike might look like 10% but only on a part of your salary. Check your increment letter carefully.

## 6. How to actually increase take-home

If your goal is to maximize take-home, there are only a few legitimate tricks left in 2025-26.

Ask your employer to offer NPS benefit under Section 80CCD(2), which is over and above 80C. This is tax-free up to 10% of basic and can save serious tax, but only some companies allow it.

Push for more tax-free perks: meal coupons, fuel reimbursement, phone/internet bills, LTA. These aren’t always standard, but many HRs will quietly add them if you ask.

Flexi-benefit plans can let you restructure special allowance into more tax-efficient heads. For example, split special allowance into children’s education allowance, books, or even uniform allowance (yes, even for software engineers—some companies allow it).

Don’t ignore employer PF. It’s forced savings, but it’s also locked. If you’re tight on cash flow, ask for a lower basic (some startups allow this), but remember, long-term compounding in EPF is powerful.

## 8. Take-home confusion: common scenarios

### Salary structure changes every year

Every April, HR might tweak your salary split to optimize for tax rules, PF limits, or compliance. This is routine. Don’t panic if HRA or special allowance jumps or drops—the total CTC usually doesn’t change, just the buckets.

### Take-home drops after a hike

This happens when your hike pushes you into a higher tax slab, but deductions (like standard deduction) stay flat. My friend Swati in Gurgaon got a 10% hike, but her net monthly only went up by 5%. Welcome to the 25% income tax club.

### Rent receipts and HRA exemption

If you don’t submit rent receipts, your HRA is fully taxable. Even if your CTC shows ₹5 lakh HRA, the tax exemption only comes if you can prove rent. Use the [HRA exemption calculator](/salary/hra-calculator/) and keep those receipts.

### Bonus and variable pay month

When annual bonus hits, tax spikes that month. Your in-hand salary for the bonus month can drop by 30–40% due to TDS on the lump sum. Plan your spends accordingly.

### Loans and EMI eligibility

Banks don’t care about your CTC. They look at monthly in-hand for EMI eligibility. Use our [EMI calculator](/loan/emi-calculator/) with your actual take-home, not your CTC, to avoid shock at loan rejection.
