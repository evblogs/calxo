My friend Neha landed a ₹20 lakh CTC job in Bengaluru last July. She called me after her first salary hit the account, frustrated because her offer letter said twenty lakhs, but her bank balance told a different story. “Bhai, I should be getting more than ₹1.6 lakh every month, right? Where is my money going?” She’d budgeted for rent, a new iPhone, and a Goa trip, but her actual take-home was way less than she’d dreamed. If you see yourself in Neha’s confusion, welcome to the most misunderstood part of Indian salary math: the yawning gap between CTC and what you actually get.

## 1. The five layers between offer letter and bank account

First, the CTC you see in the offer letter isn't remotely what lands in your account. There are five thick walls between your HR’s promise and your UPI alert. Each one slices off a chunk of your “lakhs per annum” before you ever see it.

The first layer is the employer’s side of PF and gratuity. These are part of your CTC, but you never get them directly. Instead, ₹1,20,000 for employer PF and another ₹48,100 as gratuity are stashed away for future you. The second wall? Your own PF deduction. Another ₹1,20,000 is pulled from your gross. Third, professional tax. In Karnataka, it’s only ₹2,400 a year, but in Mumbai it’s steeper. Fourth, of course, is income tax. The new regime with zero exemptions and a flat ₹50,000 standard deduction means most people pay a hefty chunk. Finally, there are hidden “special allowance” chunks that pad CTC but don’t feel real.

By the time all these filters do their job, that ₹20 lakh CTC is sliced down to a take-home that’s just 76.5% of what you expected. Most freshers and even mid-career folks are shocked. Don’t be. This is how every corporate salary is built.

## 2. What CTC actually contains

CTC, or Cost to Company, is a bundle of real and not-so-real money. The pieces? Let’s break it.

Basic salary always forms the backbone. Here, it’s ₹10,00,000, exactly half the CTC, which is typical for most Indian IT and consulting roles. HRA comes next, calculated at 40% of basic, so ₹4,00,000 if you’re in a city like Bengaluru or Pune. Then, there’s the special allowance. This is the “filler” HR uses to bridge the gap between the real components and the promised CTC. For our example, it’s ₹3,80,700.

Employer PF is quietly added, but never directly paid out. That’s ₹1,20,000. Gratuity provision, a silent CTC inflator, is ₹48,100. You only see it after 5 years, if you stick around. If you switch jobs every two years, this is vaporware. All these pieces add up to the headline CTC, but only the “annual gross” (₹18,31,900, after cutting employer-side benefits) is ever paid out by payroll.

I’ve seen HRs quietly bump up special allowance and lower HRA if you’re relocating. Watch out for that. If you want to check your own split, try the [take-home salary calculator](/salary/takehome-calculator/).

## 4. How the regime choice changes take-home

The “old vs new regime” tax debate is alive and kicking. With the new regime’s standard deduction and no exemptions, most people just pick whatever HR puts in their onboarding form. Don’t. For a ₹20 lakh CTC, the difference is often small but can matter if you’re a heavy investor in 80C, NPS, or have big home loan interest.

Let’s see the numbers for both regimes, assuming typical salaried deductions (₹1.5L 80C, ₹50K NPS, ₹25K 80D):

Old regime is only slightly better, IF you do full 80C + NPS + insurance. Most people are too lazy to max out these. If you have no investments, the new regime is simpler and often cheaper. ## 5. Five things HR won’t tell you

First, “variable pay” is counted in CTC, but you may never get the full amount. If your company’s performance is poor, or your manager is stingy, expect a lower payout. Neha’s ₹2 lakh variable was paid at 75% last year, so she lost ₹50,000 straight out of her “CTC”.

Second, EPF is taxable above ₹2.5 lakh annual employee contribution, including employer share. Most people at this CTC don’t hit the limit, but if you have voluntary PF, watch out. Third, gratuity is only paid after 5 years of *continuous* service. If you switch jobs, your ₹48,100 per year just becomes a line item in HR’s Excel. Fourth, “special allowance” is fully taxable and offers zero tax breaks. Fifth, some companies sneak in medical insurance premiums and even canteen subsidies into CTC, but you never see the cash.

Don’t blindly trust the breakup. Ask for a *detailed* salary structure, and read every line. If you see “retention bonus,” treat it as fake money until it’s actually paid.

## 6. How to actually increase take-home

If you want a higher take-home, you have to work the system. Employer NPS is one of the best tricks. Up to 10% of basic salary contributed by employer to NPS is not taxed in your hands and isn’t part of 80C. If your company allows it, route part of your CTC here.

Food coupons like Sodexo or Zeta save tax, up to ₹50 per meal, ₹2,200 per month. Not massive, but worth it if you eat out. Flexi-benefits for phone, internet, fuel, and books can be structured for tax savings, but only if your HR is flexible. Some companies allow you to shift special allowance into these heads. Do it.

Don’t opt for “optimum” PF. Stick to statutory 12% of basic, unless you want forced savings. If you’re renting, max out your HRA claim with real rent receipts. The [HRA exemption calculator](/salary/hra-calculator/) will show the impact. Most critical: always negotiate for higher basic or higher HRA when you join. Once the split is set, HR rarely changes it unless you threaten to quit.

## 8. FAQ section

### Income tax calculation for ₹20 lakh CTC

For FY 2025-26 under the new regime, after the ₹50,000 standard deduction, your taxable income is ₹17,56,900. Tax is slabbed like this: ₹0 up to ₹4 lakh, ₹20,000 for next ₹4 lakh (5%), ₹40,000 for next ₹4 lakh (10%), ₹75,000 for next ₹5 lakh (15%), and ₹35,000 for the last ₹1.57 lakh (20%). That totals ₹1,70,000, plus 4% cess, giving ₹1,76,800. HR usually rounds this up for TDS, so actual in-hand may vary slightly each month.

### Employer PF and gratuity: do you ever see it?

Employer PF (₹1,20,000 per year) is deposited into your EPF account, visible on EPFO portal. You get it back when you switch jobs or retire, but it’s not cash-in-hand monthly. Gratuity (₹48,100 per year) is only paid if you complete 5 years at the company, and is calculated on last drawn basic salary, not CTC.

### Professional tax: why does it change by state?

Professional tax is a state subject. In Karnataka, it’s ₹2,400 per year, deducted monthly. In Maharashtra, it’s higher for higher salaries, and some states have no professional tax at all. If you relocate for work, your take-home will change even if your CTC remains constant.

### Special allowance: why so high?

Special allowance is a “balancing” figure in Indian salary structures. After accounting for basic, HRA, employer PF, and gratuity, what’s left to reach the promised CTC is just dumped here. It’s fully taxable, and gives no tax benefits. If your special allowance is more than 25-30% of CTC, HR is just padding numbers.

### Should you invest more in 80C to save tax?

If you’re in the old regime, maxing out 80C (PF, ELSS, home loan principal, etc.) saves up to ₹46,800 tax. In the new regime, 80C is ignored, so SIP karo for your goals, not for tax. If your employer offers NPS, that’s a separate ₹50,000 deduction (80CCD(1B)), worth using in either regime. Use the [SIP calculator](/investment/sip-calculator/) to plan.
