## The zero-tax reality for ₹10L earners

Let’s get one thing clear right at the start: if your total CTC is ₹10 lakh, **you will pay zero income tax under the new tax regime** for FY 2025-26. This is not a loophole, or some CA hack. It’s the law, thanks to Section 87A rebate.

Here’s why. The new regime slabs for FY 2025-26 are:

- 0% up to ₹4 lakh  
- 5% from ₹4 lakh to ₹8 lakh  
- 10% from ₹8 lakh to ₹12 lakh  

But, you also get a ₹75,000 standard deduction, which brings your taxable income down before any tax is calculated. For the classic product/startup salary structure (Structure B), your gross is about ₹9.16 lakh. Subtract the ₹75,000 standard deduction, you’re left with ₹8.41 lakh taxable income.

Here’s the key: the slab tax on this is ₹24,095. But, Section 87A gives a rebate of up to ₹60,000 if your taxable income is under ₹12 lakh. So, the entire tax gets wiped off. Your **income tax is zero**. This is the most important fact about ₹10 lakh CTC today. Don’t let anyone scare you with “tax will eat your salary” stories. At this level, you’re safe.

## Why every calculator shows a different number

Have you ever plugged your CTC into three different salary calculators and got three different take-home numbers? You’re not going mad. The culprit is the **basic salary ratio**. This is the percentage of your CTC set as “basic salary” by your employer, and it totally changes your in-hand amount.

Most IT services companies (Infosys, TCS, Wipro) use a 40% basic structure. Product companies and startups (Flipkart, Ola, Razorpay) often use 50% basic. This one tweak changes how much is cut for PF, HRA, and even gratuity. That’s why your cousin in Pune with the same ₹10L CTC as you in Hyderabad can have a different take-home.

Let’s see the two popular ₹10L structures side by side:

| Component          | Structure A (IT Services, 40% Basic) | Structure B (Product, 50% Basic) |
|--------------------|--------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Basic Salary**   | ₹4,00,000                         | ₹5,00,000                     |
| **HRA**            | ₹2,00,000 (50% of basic, metro)   | ₹2,00,000 (40% of basic)      |
| **Special Allow.** | ₹3,32,760                         | ₹2,15,950                     |
| **Employer PF**    | ₹48,000 (12% of basic)            | ₹60,000 (12% of basic)        |
| **Gratuity**       | ₹19,240                           | ₹24,050                       |
| **Annual Gross**   | ₹9,32,760                         | ₹9,15,950                     |
| **Monthly Take-home** | ₹73,530                | ₹71,129                |

The higher the basic, the more gets locked up in PF and gratuity, which you don’t see in your bank account every month. This is the main reason calculators can differ by ₹2,000-4,000 per month for the same ₹10L CTC.

## Old regime vs new regime for ₹10L CTC

Most freshers and even mid-level folks stick to the new regime. The reason is simple: unless you have massive deductions (HRA, 80C, 80D, home loan), the new regime is always equal or better.

Let’s do the math for Structure B:

- **Gross Salary:** ₹9,15,950  
- **Standard Deduction:** ₹75,000  
- **Taxable Income:** ₹8,40,950

**Under the new regime:**  
Slab tax is ₹24,095, but Section 87A rebate (up to ₹60,000) wipes it out. **Income tax = ₹0.**

**Under the old regime, with only 80C (EPF + maybe ELSS):**  
Suppose you have ₹60,000 employee PF, and invest ₹90,000 in ELSS or PPF to max out 80C at ₹1.5 lakh. No other deductions. Your taxable income is ₹8,40,950 - ₹1,50,000 = ₹6,90,950.

Old regime slabs (assuming no cess for now):

- 0% up to ₹2.5 lakh
- 5% on ₹2.5-5 lakh = ₹12,500
- 20% on ₹5-6.9 lakh = ₹38,190

Total = ₹50,690 tax. But Section 87A rebate (old regime limit is ₹5 lakh, so not eligible) **does NOT apply**. So you pay the full tax.

**Net result:** Under the new regime, your tax is zero. Under the old regime, unless you have deductions exceeding ₹2.5-3 lakh (like HRA, 80D medical, home loan interest), you lose out.

My friend Saurabh in Chennai did the math: even with ₹1.5 lakh 80C and ₹25,000 80D (health insurance), he still paid more tax in the old regime, about ₹1,000 per month more. Only if you have a high rent (HRA exemption), max 80C, plus 80D, does old regime sometimes win, and even then, by just ₹500-1,000 per month.

## What if your ₹10L offer includes variable pay

Now comes a reality most HRs “forget” to explain. Many ₹10 lakh CTC offers include a variable/bonus portion, usually 10-20% of CTC. For example: ₹8.5 lakh fixed, ₹1.5 lakh variable (paid only if targets met, often quarterly).

Here’s how it actually works:

- Your monthly base take-home is calculated on the fixed ₹8.5 lakh, not the full CTC. That means your monthly in-hand drops to ₹62,000-65,000.
- If you meet targets and get the full 1.5 lakh variable, it’s paid as a quarterly or annual bonus, not monthly. Each quarter, you might get a one-time credit of ₹30,000-35,000.
- If you don’t hit the targets, you might get only 50% or nothing at all.

Most people only look at annual CTC and expect a higher monthly salary. Don’t. Always ask HR for the “fixed monthly gross” and “variable component” split.

## HRA exemption calculation for renters

Every blog says “HRA is tax-free if you pay rent,” but nobody actually shows the math. Let’s fix that for good.

Suppose you are on Structure B, working in Bengaluru (a metro), and paying ₹15,000 rent per month. Here’s how HRA exemption works in the old regime:

- **HRA received**: ₹2,00,000 per year (₹16,667/month)
- **50% of basic (metro)**: ₹2,50,000 per year
- **Actual rent minus 10% basic**: (₹1,80,000 rent paid) - (₹50,000) = ₹1,30,000

The exemption is the minimum of these three: ₹2,00,000 (HRA received), ₹2,50,000 (50% of basic), ₹1,30,000 (rent minus 10% basic). So, **₹1,30,000 of HRA is tax-free**, the remaining ₹70,000 is taxable.

If you were in Pune or Chennai (non-metro), it’s 40% of basic, so only ₹2,00,000 (not ₹2,50,000) comes into play.

## Five ways to increase take-home without a hike

You can’t always negotiate a hike, but you can tweak your pay structure to boost your take-home. Here are five **real** levers. HRs rarely tell freshers, but you can push for these.

1. **NPS (National Pension Scheme) contribution**: Ask for part of your CTC to go into NPS under Section 80CCD(2). This is over and above 80C, and can shave off more taxable income (old regime). Some startups offer this as a benefit.

2. **Cap your PF to statutory minimum**: Some companies allow you to cap your PF deduction to the statutory limit (₹1,800/month), which means more in your pocket monthly. But you’ll get less in your PF corpus later. Decide based on your immediate vs. long-term needs. Use our [EPF calculator](/salary/epf-calculator/) to see the effect.

3. **Meal cards or Sodexo**: If your employer offers meal cards (up to ₹2,200 per month), this is tax-free and boosts your take-home. It’s small, but every rupee counts.

4. **LTA (Leave Travel Allowance)**: If you travel for vacation within India and keep bills, LTA can be claimed tax-free (old regime). This is usually part of your CTC, but many people ignore the claim.

5. **Flexible benefits (choose your own perks)**: Some companies let you allocate part of your CTC to tax-free reimbursements like books, fuel, internet, or even phone bills. Don’t just leave it as “special allowance,” split it up to maximize tax savings.

These tweaks can mean an extra ₹1,000-2,000 per month in your account, without begging for a hike.

## FAQ: ₹10 lakh CTC take-home, tax, and salary structure

### 10 lakh CTC means how much monthly salary in-hand?

Typically, ₹10 lakh CTC gives you a monthly take-home of about ₹71,129 if your CTC is fully fixed and you’re in a metro city with professional tax. The exact figure depends on your basic salary ratio and the city’s professional tax. If your CTC includes variable pay, your fixed monthly in-hand will be lower, and the variable will come as lump sum credits on target achievement.

### Why is my take-home so much less than my CTC?

CTC is a misleading number. It includes employer PF, gratuity, sometimes insurance premiums, and even variable pay that you may not actually receive. Your take-home is what you get after PF deduction, professional tax, and income tax (which is zero for most ₹10L earners in FY 2025-26). The basic salary ratio also affects how much gets locked up in PF.

### Do I need to file ITR if my tax is zero?

Yes, you should still file your ITR (Income Tax Return) even if your tax liability is zero due to the Section 87A rebate. It’s required for many things: getting a loan, visa, or just staying compliant. The ITR helps you claim refunds if excess TDS was cut, and acts as income proof.

### Should I choose the old or new tax regime for ₹10L CTC?

For most people with ₹10L CTC, the new regime is better unless you have high deductions: maxed out 80C, high HRA exemption (paying ₹20,000+ rent in a metro), and claim 80D or home loan interest. Even then, the difference is marginal, often just ₹500-1,000 per month. For a fresher or single earner with average deductions, new regime wins hands down. Play with your own numbers on our [old vs new tax regime comparison](/blog/old-vs-new-tax-regime-fy-2025-26/).

### How does variable pay affect my monthly take-home?

If your CTC is split as, say, ₹8.5L fixed and ₹1.5L variable, your monthly take-home is calculated only on ₹8.5L. The variable is paid out quarterly or yearly if targets are met. So you’ll see a lower in-hand every month, and lump-sum credits if you meet targets. Always ask HR for the “fixed gross” amount.

### How is gratuity calculated and do I get it every month?

Gratuity is a part of CTC, but you don’t get it monthly. It’s a statutory benefit paid when you complete 5 years with your employer. For ₹10L CTC, it’s typically ₹19,240-24,050 per year, depending on your basic. If you leave before 5 years, you get nothing.
