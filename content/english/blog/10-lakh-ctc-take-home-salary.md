My friend Ajay landed his first job in Bengaluru last July, fresh out of college, and the whole family did a small celebration when his offer letter showed ₹10 lakh CTC. His uncle immediately did the mental math: "That's ₹83,333 per month, Ajay beta." Everyone nodded. But when Ajay’s first salary hit his bank account, the number staring back was ₹71,300. Not even close to that ₹83k. He called me up, sounding half-panicked: "Bhai, my salary is missing! Where did my money go?" This isn’t just Ajay. Every fresher, every job hopper in India learns the CTC-to-take-home lesson the hard way. Let’s break down exactly what happens between the offer letter and your account, so the next time someone throws a big CTC at you, you can see through the HR magic.

## 1. The five layers between offer letter and bank account

First, there’s the illusion that CTC equals salary. Don’t. Seriously. CTC is everything your employer pays for you, not everything you get. The first cut is employer PF. That 12 percent of basic isn’t paid to you, it goes straight to EPF. Next, gratuity provision is included in your CTC, even if you never see it unless you stick around for five years. Then, annual gross salary is what your payroll team actually credits as income. But wait, there’s more. From gross, your employee-side PF is deducted. Professional tax hits you, state-wise. Income tax, if any, is chopped off before you ever see your salary. So, five layers: employer PF, gratuity, employee PF, professional tax, income tax. The final amount is your actual take-home.

## 2. What CTC actually contains

Every ₹10 lakh CTC is a cocktail of fixed and not-so-fixed components. Basic salary is usually pegged at 50% of CTC, so here it’s ₹5,00,000. HRA is 40% of basic for non-metros, so ₹2,00,000. Special allowance is the balancing figure, the HR favorite to fill gaps and adjust for tax efficiency — ₹2,16,527 in this case. Employer PF, a fixed 12% of basic, is ₹60,000. Gratuity is computed at 4.81% of basic, here ₹24,050. Add it all up, and you hit ₹10 lakh exactly. But only the gross salary — ₹9,15,950 — is paid out in payroll. The rest is statutory or future benefit. No, you can’t ask HR to give you employer PF in cash. Try, and watch them laugh.

## 4. How the regime choice changes take-home

This is one of those years where the new regime is a game changer for mid-level CTCs. The 87A rebate is ₹60,000, and for taxable incomes up to ₹12 lakh, you pay zero tax under the new regime. No kidding. Old regime? Tax still bites, unless you max out 80C, 80D, HRA, and other exemptions.

Let’s compare both regimes for this ₹10 lakh CTC:

| Regime           | Taxable Income | Income Tax | Annual Take-home (₹) | Monthly Take-home (₹) |
|------------------|---------------|------------|----------------------|-----------------------|
| New Regime       | 8,55,950      | 0          | 8,55,550             | 71,300                |
| Old Regime*      | 7,71,950      | 8,370      | 8,47,180             | 70,600                |

*Assumes you claim full 80C (PF+ELSS), standard deduction, HRA exemption (use the [HRA exemption calculator](/salary/hra-calculator/)). Still, old regime gives you ₹700 less per month — and more paperwork. Read the [old vs new tax regime article](/blog/old-vs-new-tax-regime-fy-2025-26/) if you want to see detailed pros and cons. If your CTC is ₹10 lakh, the new regime is almost always better. Don’t waste energy chasing old regime unless you have big home loan interest or crazy HRA claims.

## 5. Five things HR won't tell you

1. Variable pay is part of CTC, but not guaranteed. Festive bonus, performance bonus, referral bonus — all are usually "up to" amounts. Most people get less than the full variable.

2. Employer PF is tax-free up to ₹1.5 lakh under 80C, but interest on employee+employer PF above ₹2.5 lakh per year is now taxable. On ₹10 lakh CTC, you’re safe, but at higher bands, watch out.

3. Gratuity accrues only if you complete 5 years. Leave in 4 years, that ₹24,050 is gone. HR never mentions this during offer discussions.

4. Professional tax varies by state. Karnataka is ₹2,400 per year, Maharashtra is ₹2,500, some states have zero PT. Your city matters.

5. Flexible benefits like fuel, phone, LTA, food coupons can be offered, but most companies default to "special allowance" for simplicity. You have to push to get tax-friendly perks.

## 6. How to actually increase take-home

There’s no magic button, but a few levers work. Employer NPS is one — up to 10% of basic as employer NPS is tax-exempt beyond 80C, and not counted as income for tax. Ask your HR if they allow NPS deductions, especially if you’re planning to stick around. Food coupons: Sodexo, Zeta, or paper vouchers, up to ₹50 per meal is tax-free if given as perquisite. Flexi-benefits: structure your salary so that mobile bill, internet, LTA, uniform allowance, education allowance are paid as reimbursement, not as special allowance. Most companies don’t push this, but if you have the option, use it.

## 8. FAQs

### Tax-free salary for ₹10 lakh CTC

Yes, if you pick the new regime in FY 2025-26, your tax is zero, thanks to the 87A rebate. Your taxable income stays below ₹12 lakh, so there’s no tax cut, no TDS, nothing. If payroll is still deducting tax, ask them why.

### HRA exemption at ₹10 lakh CTC

HRA is included, but you only get exemption if you pay rent and the old regime is chosen. In the new regime, HRA exemption is not available. If your rent is high, do the math using the [HRA exemption calculator](/salary/hra-calculator/) to see if old regime makes sense.

### PF contribution and withdrawal

Both employer and employee contribute ₹60,000 each to EPF here. Withdrawals after 5 years are tax-free. If you change jobs, transfer your PF. Don’t withdraw unless you really need it — you lose compounding.

### Professional tax impact

Professional tax is ₹2,400 per year in Karnataka. Other states differ. It’s a statutory deduction, not an income tax, and shows up as a separate line item on your payslip.

### Special allowance meaning

Special allowance is the balancing figure in your CTC, after all other components are fixed. It’s fully taxable, no exemptions. Some companies use it as a catch-all for everything not named.
